In halls of marble, cold and wide,
Where flags like restless oceans glide,
And echoes of ambition rise
Beneath the watch of ancient skies,
There walks a game both old and new—
A stage where promises pass through.

Politics, the silver tongue,
The anthem forever half-unsung,
A field where dreams and power meet,
Where dusty hope and leather seats
Are stitched together thread by thread
By words the hungry nation fed.

A child once stood beside the street,
Barefoot against the summer heat,
And watched a convoy speeding by
Like thunder crossing through the sky.
He asked his father, “Who are they?”
The father sighed and turned away.

“They are the voices of the land,
The ones who claim to understand.
Some build bridges, schools, and light,
Some disappear into the night.
Some carry truth within their chest,
Some wear deception like a vest.”

And so the child began to learn
How nations break and how they turn;
How speeches bloom like fields in spring,
How crowds rejoice when leaders sing,
Yet silence falls when seasons change
And loyalty begins to estrange.

In every city’s crowded square,
There hangs ambition in the air.
The banners wave, the slogans roar,
The hopeful gather by the door.
Each candidate with polished grin
Declares the age they will begin.

One speaks of justice, loud and bright,
Of equal laws and workers’ rights.
One vows to guard tradition’s flame,
To honor blood, belief, and name.
Another dreams of markets free,
Of wealth unchained across the sea.

And people listen, tired yet wise,
With generations in their eyes.
For every promise loudly made
Reminds them of another fade—
A broken road, a vanished fund,
A war declared, a freedom shunned.

Politics is not just crowns,
Or kings who wear ceremonial gowns.
It lives in buses, fields, and bread,
In every tear a mother shed.
It hides in laws both small and vast,
In what is future, what is past.

It lives inside the farmer’s rain,
The student drowning under strain,
The laborer whose aching hands
Still build the towers of the land.
It breathes within the doctor’s ward
Where medicine is priced and stored.

Some enter politics with fire,
With honest hearts and fierce desire.
They dream of lifting up the poor,
Of opening each rusted door.
But power whispers soft and low
The way a poisoned river flows.

At first it asks for little things:
A favor here, a meeting ringed
With wealthy men in tailored coats
Who calculate through silent notes.
A compromise, a hidden deal,
A truth too dangerous to reveal.

And slowly ideals start to bend,
Not sharply break—but slowly mend
Into a shape they once opposed,
Behind secured and guarded doors.
The mirror changes day by day
Until the soul has drifted gray.

Yet not all leaders lose their way.
Some stand like mountains through decay.
Some choose the people over pride,
Keep conscience firmly by their side.
They walk through storms of hate and lies
Still holding truth before their eyes.

History remembers names
Both wrapped in honor and in flames.
The tyrant seated high in gold
Who ruled through fear and growing cold;
The rebel rising from the dust
Who spoke because he knew he must.

Empires have risen from a speech,
And fallen when they failed to reach
The wounded hearts beneath the noise,
The quiet grief of girls and boys.
No throne survives forevermore;
Time eventually levels all doors.

Democracy, that fragile glass,
Can strengthen—or can quickly pass.
It asks the people to remain
Awake against corruption’s chain.
For freedom is not simply won;
Its battle never truly done.

The vote is small—a mark, a line—
Yet carries weight through space and time.
A pencil trembling in the hand
Can redirect an entire land.
And silence too becomes a choice
When fear replaces public voice.

There are those nights before elections
Filled with anxious recollections.
The television flickers blue,
Analysts debate what’s true.
The old man prays, the young debate,
The nation balances on fate.

Then morning comes with ink-stained skin,
The counting starts, the chants begin.
Some celebrate with drums and cheers,
Some hide disappointment’s tears.
Yet underneath both loss and praise
The country walks through changing days.

Politics can birth a war
With one proud sentence shouted for
Honor, borders, faith, or land—
A signature, a raised command.
And somewhere far from royal rooms
Young soldiers march toward smoky tombs.

Mothers wait beside the gate,
Reading headlines filled with fate.
Children learn the cost of pride
When flags are wrapped around those who died.
The rulers speak of noble cause;
The people bury human loss.

Still politics can also heal.
It can rebuild what pain made real.
A treaty signed by trembling hands,
A bridge stretched out between two lands,
A law that says a child may eat,
A shelter built beside the street.

For at its best, this art of state
Can challenge hunger, lessen hate.
Can lift the weak, defend the small,
Can answer justice when they call.
The pen, the vote, the public will—
These are powers greater still.

But citizens must guard the flame,
Not worship any single name.
For blind devotion births the night
Where truth itself avoids the light.
A leader should not be a god,
Nor critics crushed beneath the rod.

Ask questions. Study. Disagree.
Protect the right of speech to be.
A nation strong is not one voice
But millions carrying their choice.
The loud, the quiet, rich, and poor
Must all stand equal at the door.

And somewhere in a future year,
Another child may stand and hear
The thunder of a convoy pass
Reflected on a window’s glass.
Perhaps they’ll ask, “What does power mean?”
And not just what is heard or seen.

Perhaps the answer then will be:
“It means responsibility.
Not palaces, nor endless fame,
Nor statues carved beside a name.
But serving people day by day
And leaving fear no place to stay.”

Until that dawn, the world spins on,
Through every dusk and every dawn.
Politics—the endless sea
Of conflict, hope, and history.
A mirror held to humankind:
Its darkest heart, its brightest mind.
